Website

The new ICSW website is actively in development with our web development partner. Below is a summary of the key decisions and standards being built into the project.

Accessibility (WCAG 2.1 AA)

Accessibility compliance is important for any higher education institution. It ensures the website is usable by people with disabilities and helps meet legal requirements. Our developer will build with WCAG best practices from day one (semantic HTML, color contrast, keyboard navigation, alt text, and more). A formal compliance audit will be conducted as a separate phase after the initial site build is complete. This approach keeps the project timeline on track while giving us full visibility into what needs addressing, rather than discovering blockers mid-build, particularly around third-party plugins, form components, and dynamic content in Bricks Builder.

Page Speed Targets

Desktop
90+
Lighthouse / PageSpeed score
Mobile
75+
Lighthouse / PageSpeed score

Our developer is confident in hitting these numbers through caching, code optimization, and image handling during development. Final scores can be influenced by server environment, hosting configuration, and third-party scripts, so a 2 to 3 point variance is normal and expected.

Responsive Design & Mobile Layouts

Our team is creating dedicated mobile layout designs for the 4 primary landing pages in Milestone 1. The developer will implement these exactly as provided. For the remaining pages, the developer will carry the patterns established in those first 4 layouts across the rest of the site for visual and behavioral consistency. We are aiming to have the first four page designs ready for review next week.

Why mobile designs matter for ICSW:
  • Your prospective students are on mobile. Google Ads data shows 65.8% of Search clicks and 86.5% of PMax clicks come from mobile devices. The majority of your paid traffic will land on a phone, and that first impression needs to be intentional, not an afterthought.
  • Desktop designs don't automatically translate. Simply shrinking a desktop layout to fit a phone screen leads to hard-to-read text, misaligned elements, and poor navigation. Dedicated mobile designs ensure forms, calls to action, and key information are easy to find and use on smaller screens.
  • Mobile experience impacts ad performance. Google factors page experience into ad quality scores. A slow or poorly laid-out mobile page can increase your cost per click and reduce the return on your ad spend, which is the opposite of what we want heading into the June 30 push.
  • Higher education applicants research on the go. Prospective grad students are often working professionals browsing programs between commitments. A seamless mobile experience reduces friction in the path from interest to application.
